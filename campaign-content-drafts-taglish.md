### Phishing Sites Targeting Philippine Businesses Surge 423% in 2025

The number of phishing websites targeting the Philippines surged 423% in 2025 — from 731 to 3,824 — according to a new report from cybersecurity firm Check Point.

The report, published in February 2026, paints a picture of what researchers call "industrialized" fraud: scam operations that don't just target individuals, but systematically impersonate businesses, brands, and government agencies at scale.

**Lahat ng metric, pataas — at ang businesses ang target**

The Check Point data comes on the heels of other alarming findings:

- The GSMA's 2025 ASEAN Consumer Scam Report found that 52% of Filipinos have been scammed at least once — 7 percentage points above the regional average.
- Whoscall's 2025 Philippines Scam Report showed risky URLs surging from 13,602 to 49,431 within the year — halos quadruple sa loob ng isang taon.
- The PNP logged over 1,525 online selling scam cases in 2025 alone.

Hindi na ito isolated incidents. Part na ito ng trend na ilang taon nang bumibilis.

**Ang pangalawang biktima na walang nagsasabi: ang business na ini-impersonate**

Most scam coverage focuses on consumer victims — the people who click the wrong link or pay the wrong account. Pero may pangalawang biktima na bihirang mapag-usapan: 'yung business na ginagaya.

When a scammer creates a fake Facebook page using a real business's name, photos, and branding, 'yung totoong business ang nagbabayad ng presyo. Customers lose trust. Bookings get questioned. Payment channels get flagged. Reputation damage is often permanent.

Check Point's report noted a 37% rise in fake brand and executive profiles on social media — from 940 to 1,291. Ibig sabihin, mas maraming businesses ang kinokopya, hindi mas kaunti.

**Social media na ang #1 channel**

The GSMA report identified social media as the leading channel for scams in the Philippines, surpassing SMS and email. Malaking bagay ito kasi ibig sabihin, nag-ooperate na ang mga scammer sa mismong platform kung saan nakikipag-interact ang mga businesses sa customers nila.

A fake Facebook page doesn't need to look perfect. Kailangan lang na mukhang totoo para sa customer na nagmamadali at hindi na nagdouble-check. And with professional-looking templates, purchased likes, and stolen photos available for almost nothing, halos zero na ang barrier para gumawa ng convincing na peke.

**May mga bagong batas na — pero hindi makasabay ang enforcement**

The Philippine government has responded with legislation. The Anti-Financial Account Scamming Act (AFASA), enacted in 2024, targets digital financial fraud. The Internet Transactions Act of 2023 gave the DTI authority to issue takedown orders against fraudulent online sellers.

Pero reactive ang enforcement — ina-address nito ang scams pagkatapos na mangyari. The question facing Philippine businesses is increasingly not "will we get impersonated?" but "kapag nangyari, makikita ba ng customers mo ang pagkakaiba?"

**Hindi na sapat ang mga lumang paraan para patunayan na legit ka**

With scammers scaling operations faster than enforcement can keep up, lumilipat na ang burden sa mga businesses mismo. Companies that invest in visible, verifiable proof of their identity will be better positioned to maintain customer trust in an environment where fakes are becoming indistinguishable from the real thing.

The 423% surge isn't just a statistic. It's a signal that the old ways of proving legitimacy — a professional-looking website, a Facebook page with likes, an SSL certificate — hindi na sapat.

*Sources: Check Point Philippine Threat Landscape Report 2025, GSMA ASEAN Consumer Scam Report 2025, Whoscall PH Scam Report 2025, PNP-ACG*

### How Fake Facebook Pages Are Stealing Customers from Real Philippine Resorts

When guests started showing up at a Bantayan Island resort expecting their bookings to be confirmed, walang ideya ang staff kung ano ang pinag-uusapan nila.

The guests had sent deposits through GCash — to a Facebook page that used the resort's name, photos, and location. The page had over 50,000 likes. 'Yung totoong resort page? Offline noon.

Over 200 guests were affected. The resort estimated losses at P500,000 from that single fake page.

Hindi ito isolated incident. Part ito ng pattern na tumatama sa tourism businesses sa buong bansa.

**Ang resort na hindi naman talaga existing**

In La Union's popular surf town of San Juan, a Facebook page called "Cabanas La Union" amassed 8,000 likes. Magagandang beachfront photos, Urbiztondo address, at tumatanggap ng bookings with upfront payment.

Pagdating ng guests, wala doon.

The resort didn't exist. Wala sa Google Maps. Wala sa DTI's Business Name Registration portal. Wala kahit saan. Ang mga photos, ninakaw pa sa isang beach resort sa Batangas — daan-daang kilometro ang layo.

Verafiles fact-checked and confirmed na fabricated lahat. The page was created in February 2024 and changed its name twice para makaiwas sa detection bago na-take down.

**Nagbabayad ng ads para sa pekeng resorts**

In Batangas, a well-known beach resort discovered that scammers weren't just creating fake pages — nagbabayad pa sila ng Facebook ads gamit ang pangalan at photos ng resort para kolektahin ang bookings.

The fact that scammers are investing money in advertising fake businesses shows how profitable the operation has become. Kung nagga-gastos sila sa ads, malaki siguro ang return on investment.

**Cebu tourism nag-issue ng joint warning — isang syndicate ang likod ng maraming fake hotels**

By late 2025 and into 2026, the Cebu hotel industry and provincial tourism office issued a joint warning. Fake Facebook pages were mimicking real hotels across the province, with scammers using official photos and offering realistic rates — hindi suspiciously mura — para hindi mahuli.

Local authorities believe a large syndicate is behind the coordinated attacks. GMA News reported 89 online travel scam reports filed in just the first five months of 2025.

**Bakit paulit-ulit itong gumagana**

The scam works because of a simple trust gap: walang madaling paraan para ma-verify ng customer na talagang pag-aari ng business 'yung Facebook page.

Facebook's own verification systems are inconsistent. A blue checkmark on Facebook or Instagram is not a guarantee of business identity — it's a paid subscription. Small and medium businesses don't have the brand recognition that makes fakes immediately obvious.

So customers rely on proxies: the number of likes, the quality of photos, the responsiveness of the page. Alam ito ng mga scammer, at niga-game nila ang bawat isa sa mga signals na 'yan.

**Ano ang magagawa ng resort owners ngayon**

1. **Panatilihing active ang official pages mo.** An offline or inactive page makes it easier for fakes to fill the vacuum. Post regularly, kahit off-season.

2. **I-claim ang business mo sa Google Maps at iba pang directories.** The more places your real business information appears, the harder it is for fakes to seem legit.

3. **Sabihin sa customers mo kung paano mag-verify.** Include your official booking channels in your bio, website, and every communication. Maging explicit: "We only accept bookings through [specific channels]."

4. **I-report agad ang fake pages.** File with Facebook, DTI, and PNP-ACG. Document everything — screenshots, payment evidence, customer complaints.

5. **Bantayan ang copies.** Search your business name on Facebook regularly. Set up Google Alerts for your brand name.

The tourism industry is one of the Philippines' most important economic sectors. Pero hangga't hindi madaling makita ng customers kung alin ang totoo at peke, every legitimate resort, hotel, and travel business is at risk.

### The P480 Billion Problem: Why Online Scams Are a Business Crisis, Not Just a Consumer One

The Philippines loses an estimated P480 billion (USD 8.29 billion) to scams every year. That's 1.9% of GDP, placing the country second globally in terms of digital fraud rate.

The number comes from ScamWatch HQ's 2025 analysis, and nakakagulat talaga. P1.3 billion per day. P55 million per hour. Every hour.

Pero ito ang nawawala sa headline: most of this money was meant for real businesses. Real resorts. Real online shops. Real restaurants. Hindi gumawa ng demand ang scammer — in-intercept lang niya.

**Ang business victim na walang nagsasabi**

When a scammer creates a fake page that copies a real business, dalawang tao ang nasasaktan.

The first is the customer who pays the wrong account. 'Yan ang biktimang kinukuwento ng balita.

The second is the business whose name was used. 'Yan ang biktimang hindi pinag-uusapan.

The business doesn't just lose the revenue from that transaction. Nawawalan sila ng isang bagay na mas mahirap sukatin: trust. Customers who've been burned — or who've heard about someone being burned — nagsisimulang mag-question sa lahat. "Ito ba 'yung totoong page?" "Ito ba 'yung totoong GCash number?" "Sa iba na lang kaya ako mag-book?"

Every hesitation is a potential lost sale. At nag-co-compound ang erosion ng trust over time.

**Tumatakbo sa ninakaw na identity ang scam economy**

Online scams aren't random. May business model sila:

1. **Kopyahin** ang identity ng totoong business — name, photos, logo, branding.
2. **Bumuo** ng credibility — purchased likes, stolen reviews, professional-looking page.
3. **I-intercept** ang customer demand — same product, same price, same location.
4. **Kolektahin** ang payment — e-wallet, bank transfer, cash on delivery with substituted accounts.
5. **Maglaho** — i-delete ang page, gumawa ng bago, ulitin.

Ang totoong business ang gumagawa ng lahat ng marketing. Ang scammer ang nag-a-ani ng demand.

Check Point's 2025 report documented a 37% increase in fake brand profiles on social media. The GSMA found that social media has overtaken SMS as the #1 scam channel in the Philippines. PNP identity theft cases more than doubled from 2022 to 2023 — from 1,402 to 2,999.

**Tourism, e-commerce, F&B, banking — walang industriyang safe**

Some industries are hit harder than others:

**Tourism and hospitality** face the highest volume. Fake booking pages for resorts in Bantayan Island, La Union, Batangas, and across Cebu have become so common that the Cebu provincial tourism office issued a joint warning with the hotel industry.

**Online sellers and e-commerce** deal with fake storefronts. Scammers register lookalike domains — adding dashes, hyphens, or extra words to mimic major e-commerce platforms — and run phishing operations. Ang Pilipinas ang may pinakamataas na bilang ng online shopping scams sa 11 economies sa 2023 Asia Scam Report.

**Food and restaurant businesses** see their Facebook pages cloned with fake delivery pages na tumatanggap ng advance payment para sa orders na hindi naman darating.

**Banking and financial services** contend with increasingly sophisticated phishing — scam SMS messages now appear inside legitimate GCash and Maya message threads, halos imposible nang makilala mula sa totoong notifications.

**Bakit hindi sapat ang enforcement**

The government has taken action. AFASA targets digital financial fraud. The Internet Transactions Act gave DTI takedown powers. Pero may structural mismatch: reactive ang enforcement, at mas mabilis gumalaw ang mga scammer kaysa sa mga regulator.

Filing a complaint against a fake Facebook page involves DTI, PNP-ACG, NBI-CCD, sworn statements, screenshots, at buwan-buwan na paghihintay. By the time one page gets taken down, tatlo na ang bagong lumitaw.

Hindi ibig sabihin na walang silbi ang enforcement. Ibig sabihin, hindi puwedeng umasa ang businesses sa enforcement lang.

**Ang businesses ang pangunahing biktima — at kailangan na nilang kumilos**

The conversation about scams in the Philippines is still framed as a consumer protection issue. Government warns consumers. Banks tell customers to be careful. Pero 'yung mga businesses na ginagaya, tinatrato lang na background characters sa sarili nilang kwento.

Kailangan magbago 'yan.

Businesses are the ones whose identity is being stolen. Sila ang mga may customers na ini-intercept. At sila ang nagdadala ng reputational cost kapag may naging mali.

P480 billion is a national problem. Pero ramdam ito sa level ng individual businesses — isang lost booking, isang nag-aalinlangang customer, isang nasira na reputation at a time.

The question for every business owner is: ano ang ginagawa mo para malaman ng customers mo na totoo ka?

### Why a .ph Domain Alone Isn't Enough to Prove Your Business Is Real

If you've registered a .ph domain for your business, you've already done something right. A .ph domain signals that you're a Philippine business operating under local registration. Isang level up ito mula sa generic .com o free domain.

Pero may gap between what a domain proves and what your customers need to know.

**Ano talaga ang pinapatunayan ng domain**

A .ph domain proves that someone registered the domain name through an accredited registrar. 'Yan lang.

Hindi nito pinapatunayan na na-verify ng third party ang identity ng nag-register. Hindi nito pinapatunayan na legit ang business sa likod ng domain. Hindi nito binibigyan ang customers ng paraan para i-check kung sino ka bago sila mag-transact.

SSL certificates — 'yung padlock icon sa browser — proves that the connection between the customer and the website is encrypted. Sine-secure nito ang data in transit. Pero hindi nito bini-verify kung sino 'yung nasa kabilang dulo.

So a scammer can register a domain, install an SSL certificate, and present a website that looks exactly like a legit business. Para sa customer, tama lahat: .ph domain, padlock icon, professional design.

**4 signals ang ginagamit ng customers para mag-decide kung legit — fine-fake ng scammers lahat ng 4**

The real problem isn't technology. It's human.

Customers are making split-second decisions about whether to trust an online page. Tinitingnan nila:
- May real-looking domain ba?
- Professional ba ang page?
- May reviews at likes ba?
- Tumatanggap ba ng popular payment methods?

Alam ng mga scammer ang checklist na 'to, at ino-optimize nila ang bawat item. Kaya 'yung Cabanas La Union fake resort page ay may 8,000 likes kahit hindi totoo ang lugar. Every signal that customers use to gauge trust, kaya i-fake.

#### How to Spot a Fake Business Page on Facebook

Nakakita ka ng magandang deal sa Facebook. Resort package para sa Holy Week. Food delivery business na may amazing reviews. Online store na may exact item na hinahanap mo.

Mukhang totoo ang page. May likes. May photos. Sumasagot sa messages.

Pero talagang 'yung business ba 'to?

In 2025, phishing websites targeting the Philippines surged 423%. Fake brand profiles on social media rose 37%. At nakita ng GSMA report na 52% of Filipinos have been scammed at least once — mas mataas kaysa sa halos lahat ng ASEAN country.

Karamihan ng scams na 'yan, hindi hacking ang ginamit. Kopya lang. Gagawa ang scammer ng Facebook page na mukhang totoo, gagamitin ang pangalan at photos ng totoong business, at hihintayin lang na magbayad ka.

Ganito protektahan ang sarili mo.

**1. I-check ang creation date ng page**

Puntahan ang "About" section ng page at hanapin ang "Page transparency." Ipinapakita ng Facebook kung kailan ginawa ang page.

Kung sinasabi ng business na ilang taon na silang nag-o-operate pero kahapon lang ginawa ang page, red flag 'yan. Paulit-ulit gumagawa ng bagong pages ang mga scammer — at kaya nilang bumili ng daan-daang likes overnight.

**2. Hanapin ang website link**

Kinokonek ng totoong businesses ang online channels nila. Meron silang website, o at least may link sa official pages nila sa ibang platform. Ang mga scammer, sa Facebook lang usually — mas madaling gumawa at mas mahirap i-trace ng mga biktima.

Kung walang website ang business, walang linked Instagram, at wala sa ibang lugar online maliban sa Facebook, mag-ingat ka.

**3. I-check ang payment method**

Ang mga legit na businesses, increasingly gumagamit ng business e-wallet accounts, payment links, o bank accounts sa pangalan ng business. Kung personal GCash o bank transfer sa pangalan ng isang tao lang ang option, bagalan mo.

Hindi ito definitive — may mga maliliit na businesses na legit na ganito talaga ang operation. Pero combined with other red flags, sign ito na kailangan munang i-verify bago magbayad.

**4. I-search ang business name**

I-Google ang business name. I-search sa Facebook mismo. Kung may makita kang maraming pages na same name at similar content, isa sa kanila ay malamang peke. Tingnan kung aling page ang mas matagal, mas consistent ang posts, at may actual website link.

**5. I-check kung may verification badge mula sa trusted source**

Ito ang pinaka-reliable na check. Ang ibang businesses ngayon ay may Verified by dotPH badge sa website nila — verification seal mula sa official .ph domain registry. Kapag na-click mo ang badge, dadalhin ka sa public verification page kung saan makikita mo ang verified links ng business: totoong Facebook page, totoong payment channels, at totoong website.

Kaya ng scammer magkopya ng photos at bumili ng likes. Hindi nila kaya kopyahin ang verification page sa dotPH.

**Ano ang gagawin kung sa tingin mo peke ang page**

- **Huwag magbayad.** Kung may kakaiba, trust your gut.
- **I-report ang page.** Gamitin ang "Report Page" feature ng Facebook at piliin ang "Scam or fraud."
- **Balaan ang iba.** I-share ang experience mo. Habang mas maraming nagsasalita, mas mabilis na nade-down ang fake pages.

Gumagaling ang mga scammer. Pero kaya mo rin.

**Step 1: Hanapin ang badge**

#### Before You Send Money: The 3-Step Check

"Ito ba 'yung totoong GCash mo?"

Kung bumili ka na sa Facebook page o online store sa Pilipinas, malamang na-send mo na 'yang message. At malamang ang sagot ng seller: "Opo, 'yan po ang official GCash namin."

At naniwala ka. Kasi ano pa bang magagawa mo?

Ito ang problema: 'yung scammer na nagpapatakbo ng fake page, ganoon din ang isasagot sa'yo. "Opo, 'yan po ang official GCash namin." Magalang. Mabilis. May smiley face pa.

Ang tanong na "Ito ba 'yung totoong GCash mo?" hindi ka niyan pinoprotektahan. Tinatanong mo ang potentially fake page na i-confirm na totoo ang fake payment link nila. Palaging oo ang sagot.

May mas magandang paraan.

**The 3-Step Check**

**Step 1: Hanapin ang verification page ng business**

Bago ka magpadala ng pera, i-check kung may Verified by dotPH badge ang business sa website nila. I-click — dapat dalhin ka sa verification page nila sa official .ph domain registry.

Kung hindi mo makita ang badge, i-search ang business sa dotPH directly.

**Step 2: I-check ang verified payment links**

Sa verification page, naka-list ang official payment channels ng business — GCash numbers, bank accounts, payment links. Na-confirm na ng dotPH ang mga 'to.

Isulat o i-screenshot ang verified GCash number.

**Step 3: I-compare**

Pareho ba ang GCash number na binigay sa'yo ng seller at 'yung nasa verification page nila?

- **Match:** Sa totoong business ka nagbabayad. Magpadala with confidence.
- **Hindi match:** Tumigil. Huwag magpadala ng pera. Baka peke ang page na kausap mo.

**Kaya ng scammer gumawa ng fake page sa 10 minutes — pero hindi nila kaya ang registry verification**

Kaya ng scammer gumawa ng fake Facebook page sa loob ng 10 minutes. Kaya nilang kopyahin ang pangalan, photos, at customer service style.

Pero hindi nila kaya gumawa ng verification page sa dotPH. Kailangan nun ng pag-submit ng totoong identity documents sa official .ph registry. Na-verify ito ng third party — hindi self-reported.

Kapag chine-check mo ang verification page, hindi mo tinatanong ang seller kung totoo sila. Chine-check mo sa organization na nagma-manage ng bawat .ph domain sa bansa.

**Paano kung hindi pa verified ang business?**

Kung walang verification page ang business, hindi ibig sabihin na scam sila. Bago pa lang ang verification, at maraming legit na businesses ang hindi pa nag-sign up.

Pero kung magpapadala ka na ng pera at walang paraan para i-verify ang payment link, mag-extra precaution:
- Tawagan ang business directly (hanapin ang number sa Google, hindi sa Facebook page).
- I-check ang website nila para sa official payment information.
- Tanungin kung Verified by dotPH sila — free 'yan, at walang rason ang legit business para umiwas.

Ang magtanong sa seller ay hindi check. Pag-asa lang 'yon.

Ang mag-check ng verified payment links sa dotPH — 'yon ang check.

#### Tell Your Nanay: A Family Guide to Avoiding Online Scams

'Yung nanay mo, nasa Facebook. Ilang taon na. Nagfo-follow ng recipe pages, nagpa-padala ng Viber stickers, at — recently — bumibili na ng mga bagay online.

Nakakita siya ng magandang deal sa isang kitchen gadget. Magandang online store. GCash payment. Nagpadala siya ng pera.

Hindi dumating ang item.

Hindi niya sinabi sa'yo ng dalawang linggo. Nahiya siya.

Libo-libong beses nangyayari 'tong kwentong 'to bawat buwan sa Pilipinas. Sa lahat ng edad — pero ang mga nakatatandang family members ang partikular na vulnerable, kasi 'yung mga signals na pinagtitiwalaaan nila (mukhang professional ang page, maraming likes, mabilis sumagot) ay 'yun mismo ang fine-fake ng mga scammer.

52% of Filipinos have been scammed at least once. Marami sa kanila ang nasa family group chat mo ngayon.

**Bakit kailangan ng pamilya mo ang tulong mo**

Nagbago na ang scam landscape. Hindi na lang 'yung obvious na "you won a million pesos" messages. Ngayon:

- **Fake resort pages** na kamukha talaga ng totoo — same name, same photos, same location sa map. Isang fake resort page sa Bantayan Island, may 50,000 likes. Mahigit 200 pamilya ang nagbayad.
- **Fake online stores** na may professional photos at realistic pricing. Hindi suspiciously mura — normal lang, kaya hindi mo na ikinoquest.
- **Fake payment links** na lumalabas sa mismong message thread ng totoong GCash notifications mo. Binabalaan ng CICC na ang scam SMS ay pumasok na sa legit e-wallet threads.

Hindi makikita ng nanay mo ang mga 'to sa tingin lang. Walang kaya.

**3 bagay na ituro sa pamilya mo**

Hindi mo kailangang gawing paranoid ang pamilya mo. Kailangan mo lang silang bigyan ng ISANG simpleng habit.

**1. "Bago ka magbayad, mag-check muna."**

Ituro ang phrase. Gawing reflex. Bago sila magpadala ng pera sa kahit anong business online — resort, online store, food delivery — huminto at mag-check.

**2. "Hanapin ang Verified by dotPH badge."**

Kung may website ang business, hanapin ang badge. I-click. Dadalhin ka sa verification page sa dotPH website kung saan makikita mo ang totoong links ng business — totoong Facebook, totoong GCash, totoong website.

Kung 'yung links ay match sa page na binibilhan nila, safe. Kung hindi, tumigil.

**3. "Walang badge? Tumawag muna bago magbayad."**

Kung walang badge ang business (marami pa lang bago sa verification), huwag basta magtiwala sa Facebook page. Hanapin ang phone number ng business sa Google — hindi sa page mismo — at tumawag para i-confirm ang payment details.

**Paano kausapin ang pamilya**

Huwag mangaral. Huwag mong parang tanga sila. Kahit bank managers nalo-lokohan ng scammers — isang senior BPI manager halos na-scam ng fake app, ayon sa investigation ng Rappler.

Sa halip, subukan mo:

*"Ma, may nadiscover akong bagong paraan para i-check kung totoo ang isang online business bago magbayad. Patingin mo real quick."*

Ituro kung paano hanapin ang badge. Ituro kung paano i-click. Ituro ang verification page. Parang isang bagay na nadiscover ninyong dalawa — hindi isang bagay na tinuturo mo kasi vulnerable sila.

**Gawing family rule**

Ang pinaka-magandang protection ay hindi one-time lesson. Habit ito.

Idagdag sa family group chat: *"Reminder: bago magpadala ng pera online kahit sino, i-check muna ang Verified by dotPH badge. Kung walang badge, tawagan ang business directly."*

Gawing normal. Gawing expected. Gawing isang bagay na ipo-forward ng tita mo sa GROUP CHAT niya.

Ang pag-protekta sa pamilya mo mula sa scams ay hindi paranoia. Pagmamahal 'yan.
