### 5. Bantayan Island Resort Booking Scams (Sep 2025 – Feb 2026)
- **What happened:** Fake Facebook pages cloned Anika Island Resort and Kota Beach Resort — stolen photos, 50,000+ likes, payments via e-wallet. 200+ victims; one resort alone handled 100 victims who lost ~P500,000. The fake page had more likes than the real one.
- **Stage:** AWARENESS (Weeks 1–3) + CONVERSION (Weeks 5–8+)
- **Campaign approach:**
  - **Awareness:** This is the single best story for the campaign. Use it as a Facebook carousel or short video: "A fake Facebook page for this Cebu resort got 50,000 likes. The real page was offline. Guess which one customers paid?" End with: "How would YOUR customers know the difference?"
  - **Conversion:** Retell as a case study in signup-stage emails: "This resort lost P500K to scammers who copied their page. Don't wait for it to happen to you. Get verified — it's free."
- **Consumer angle:** THE anchor story for the consumer campaign. Retell from the victim's POV: "I booked a resort for our family vacation. Sent P3,500 through GCash. Showed up — and they had no record of us." The detail that the fake page had MORE likes than the real one is devastating for consumers who rely on likes as a trust signal. See Post C2 and Consumer Article 2 (full first-person composite narrative).

### 6. "Cabanas La Union" Fake Resort Scam — La Union / Elyu Area (2024–2025)
- **What happened:** A completely fabricated Facebook page called "Cabanas La Union" posed as a resort in San Juan, La Union (the Elyu surf/tourism area). It used stolen photos and videos from Seabliss Beach Resort in Lian, Batangas, claimed to be at Urbiztondo, amassed 8,000 likes, and collected payments from guests. The resort didn't exist — it wasn't on Google Maps, wasn't in DTI's Business Name Registration portal, and victims only discovered the scam after showing up. The page was created Feb 22, 2024 and changed its name twice to evade detection. **Fact-checked by Verafiles.**
- **Stage:** AWARENESS (Weeks 1–3) + CONSIDERATION (Weeks 3–5)
- **Campaign approach:**
  - **Awareness:** This story is perfect for La Union / Elyu-focused targeting. "A fake resort page in La Union got 8,000 likes using stolen photos from a Batangas resort. Guests paid and showed up to... nothing." The detail that the resort *didn't even exist* is more shocking than impersonation — it shows how easy it is to create a convincing fake.
  - **Consideration:** Use to explain why verification matters even for tourism businesses that rely on Facebook: "Your resort is real. But can your guests prove it before they send money? That's what Verified by dotPH is for."
  - **Note:** This happened in the same San Juan, La Union area as many popular surf and beach resorts — any real resort in the area could be the next target.
- **Consumer angle:** The fact that the resort DIDN'T EXIST is the most powerful consumer detail. For B2B, the story is about impersonation. For consumers, it's about fabrication — you can't even rely on "the business is real" because scammers invent entire businesses from scratch. Use in Post C3 and Consumer Article 2. Consumer framing: "This resort had 8,000 likes. It didn't exist. Before you book — how do you know the place is real?"
- **Links:**
  - [Verafiles Fact Check: FB page posing as La Union resort a SCAM](https://verafiles.org/articles/fact-check-fb-page-posing-as-la-union-resort-a-scam)
  - [GMA Regional TV: Public warned vs scams using fake resort FB pages](https://www.gmanetwork.com/regionaltv/news/111512/public-warned-vs-scams-using-fake-resort-fb-pages/story/)

- **What happened:** Scammers create domains like "shopee-ph.com" (instead of shopee.ph) and send phishing emails/messages luring users to fake sites. Others impersonate Lazada itself — not to sell products, but to run job scams and payment fraud. The Philippines had the highest number of online shopping scams among 11 economies in the 2023 Asia Scam Report.
- **Stage:** AWARENESS (Weeks 1–3)

Use to target e-commerce business owners — especially independent sellers who rely on their own .ph domain rather than marketplace platforms.
- **Consumer angle:** "That Shopee link in your inbox might not be from Shopee. Scammers register lookalike domains — shopee-ph.com instead of shopee.ph — and the difference is almost invisible." Use in Consumer Article 1 as an example of how fake websites work. Also feeds the "3 Signs a Page Is Fake" post (C8) — teach consumers to check URLs carefully.
