# NEW CONTENT: ORGANIC REELS (5 Scripts)

> **Why Reels:** Video content gets shared **1,200% more** than any other format on Facebook. Reels receive **22% higher engagement** than regular video and **3x median views**. Facebook's algorithm surfaces **50% more same-day Reels** from creators. The strategy already lists Facebook Reels as an ad placement — these extend Reels to organic.
>
> **Production note:** All scripts are designed for AI-generated motion graphics, text animations, and stock/generated visuals. No live-action filming required.
>
> **Specs:** 1080 x 1920 px (vertical), 15–30 seconds, MP4, captions required (87.64% of PH traffic is mobile — many watch without sound)

### Reel R1: "50,000 Likes. All Fake." — Scam Reveal

**Phase:** Fear Activation (Weeks 3–5)
**Based on:** Reference #5 (Bantayan Island Resort Booking Scams)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education
**Duration:** 15–20 seconds

**Script:**

```
[Frame 1 — 0:00-0:04]
Scenic beach backdrop (AI-generated or stock). Text animates in:
"A resort in Bantayan had 50,000 likes."

[Frame 2 — 0:04-0:07]
Same beach, color shifts darker. Text:
"Guests paid. They showed up."

[Frame 3 — 0:07-0:10]
Hard cut to black screen. White text slams in:
"The resort had no record of them."

[Frame 4 — 0:10-0:15]
Dark background, red accent. Stats appear one by one:
"200+ victims."
"P500,000 lost."
"One fake page."

[Frame 5 — 0:15-0:18]
Muted tone, clean text:
"Before you book — can you verify it's real?"

End card: Source: SunStar Cebu. No product branding (Fear Activation phase).
```

**Caption:**
The fake page had more likes than the real one.

Over 200 families sent deposits to a page that copied a Bantayan resort's name, photos, and location. The real resort had no idea until guests started showing up.

Send this to someone before they book their next trip.

**Posting guidance:** Best at 7–9 PM PHT weekday or 2–4 PM weekend. Pin a comment with context: "The fake page was taken down. Then a new one appeared."

### Post E1: "The Scam Copy-Paste Business Model" — Infographic

**Phase:** Fear Activation (Weeks 3–5)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education
**Format:** Static infographic (1080 x 1080 px or 1080 x 1350 px)
**Tone:** Data-driven, authoritative — the same register as Ads 1 and 4

**Primary Text:**
This is how a scammer copies your business:

1. Copy a real business page — 10 minutes
2. Buy 5,000 likes — P500
3. Steal photos from the real page — 5 minutes
4. Wait for customers to pay
5. Disappear. Create a new page. Repeat.

Total investment: P500 and 15 minutes.
Potential return: P500,000 (documented case — Bantayan resort scam).

That's why they keep doing it. And that's why the number of fake pages keeps growing.

423% more phishing sites targeting PH businesses in 2025. This isn't slowing down.

Share this with a business owner who needs to see it.

**Creative direction:** Clean infographic layout. Not a meme — designed like a data visualization or editorial graphic. Numbered steps with time/cost callouts. Muted dark palette consistent with Awareness phase visual specs. Source citation: "Check Point 2025, SunStar Cebu" in bottom corner. No product branding.

**Why this format works (playbook reference):** Infographic-style content gets shared as screenshots in Viber/Messenger GCs. The step-by-step format creates a "wow, it's that easy for scammers?" reaction that triggers sharing. Educational content gets algorithmic preference over promotional content.

### Post E2: "I Checked Everything. Still Got Scammed." — Carousel

**Phase:** Fear Activation (Weeks 3–5)
**Based on:** Reference #5 (Bantayan Island Resort Booking Scams)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education
**Format:** Carousel (5 slides, 1080 x 1080 px per slide)
**Tone:** Serious, empathetic — consumer perspective

**Primary Text:**
I checked the likes — 50,000. Looked legit.
I checked the photos — professional. Real resort photos.
I checked the reviews — positive. Recent.
I checked the response time — fast. Polite.

Still got scammed.

Because every signal I checked can be faked in 10 minutes. Likes can be purchased. Photos can be stolen. Reviews can be manufactured.

The one thing I couldn't check: whether the page actually belonged to the resort.

200+ families went through the same thing. P500,000 lost from one fake page.

Next time — don't check the likes. Check if it's verified.

**Creative direction:** Carousel slides:
1. "I checked the likes. 50,000. ✅" (clean text, muted background)
2. "I checked the photos. Professional. ✅" (same style)
3. "I checked the reviews. All positive. ✅" (same style)
4. "I checked everything." (pause beat — same background)
5. "Still got scammed." (black background, white text. Below: "Because everything I checked can be faked in 10 minutes." Source: SunStar Cebu)

No product mention on slides 1-5 — this is Fear Activation. The caption carries the bridge to verification.

**Why this format works (playbook reference):** Carousel posts outperform single image and video ads in conversion rate by 20-25%. Users swipe through an average of 3.2 cards per impression. The slide-by-slide reveal creates a narrative tension that drives engagement. This format is ideal for scam case studies.

# NEW CONTENT: INTERACTIVE POSTS (3 Posts)

> The playbook recommends interactive content 1-2x/week for algorithm fuel. The current campaign has only 1 interactive post (C1 "Spot the Fake"). These additions fit the existing 3x/week cadence and serve the strategy's content pillars.

---

### Post I1: "How Do YOU Check?" — Poll

**Phase:** Fear Activation (Weeks 3–5)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education (drives comments → algorithm engagement)
**Format:** Text post with poll options in the caption

**Primary Text:**
Before you pay an online seller, what do you check FIRST?

A) Number of likes
B) Reviews and comments
C) Page creation date
D) I just check if it "looks legit"

Drop your answer below.

(We'll share why all four of these can be faked — and what actually works — in the comments.)

**Engagement strategy:**
- Pin a reply after 2-4 hours with context: "All four can be faked. Likes can be purchased for P500. Photos can be stolen. Reviews can be manufactured. Page dates can be manipulated by renaming old pages. The one thing that can't be faked: a verification from the official .ph registry."
- Reply to individual answers with specific context (e.g., someone picks A → "A fake Bantayan resort page had 50,000 likes — more than the real one.")
- Do NOT mention Verified by dotPH in the post itself during Fear Activation phase — only in the pinned comment reply

**Why this format works (playbook reference):** Text/status posts have surprisingly high engagement rates in the Philippines. Comment-driven posts fuel the algorithm. Questions that prompt opinions generate conversation — and conversation signals are weighted heavily by Facebook's algorithm (especially back-and-forth replies).

### Post I2: "Filipino Online Shopping Bingo" — Interactive Image

**Phase:** Empowerment (Weeks 6–8)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education + Solution bridge
**Format:** Static image (1080 x 1080 px) — bingo card design

**Primary Text:**
Filipino online shopping bingo. How many can you check off?

Screenshot your card and share in the comments — or send it to your family GC.

(If you checked "Bahala na" — we need to talk. 😅)

The one square that actually protects you? "Checked for Verified by dotPH badge."

#CheckBeforeYouPay

**Bingo card squares (5x5):**

| | | | | |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| "Legit ba 'to?" | Checked the likes | Googled the business name | Asked in a Facebook group | Checked for a website |
| Sent screenshot to family GC | "Mukhang legit naman" | DM'd instead of commenting | Compared prices on 3 pages | Checked their DTI |
| Read the comments first | Asked seller "Is this your real GCash?" | **FREE SPACE: "Sana hindi scam"** | Checked page creation date | Called the business directly |

**Creative direction:** Clean bingo card design. Light, engaging visual — this is Empowerment phase, so lighter backgrounds (not the dark Fear Activation palette). The "Checked for Verified by dotPH badge" square should be visually distinct (highlighted or different color) — it's the one that actually works. Design must be screenshot-friendly (readable when forwarded in Messenger/Viber).

**Why this format works (playbook reference):** Interactive image posts drive comments ("I got 4 out of 5!") and shares (people send to friends/family to compare). The bingo format is inherently screenshot-friendly, which activates family GC distribution. The relatable squares create recognition ("Uy, ako yan!") — the meme mechanic without the meme tone.

### Post I3: "Which Page Is Real?" — Scenario Post

**Phase:** Fear Activation (Weeks 3–5)
**Content pillar:** Problem Education
**Format:** Text post or image with two options

**Primary Text:**
You found the perfect resort for Holy Week. Great photos. 12,000 likes. Accepts GCash.

But there's a second page with the same name. Same photos. 8,000 likes.

Which one is real? What would you do?

A) Book the one with more likes
B) Message both pages and compare
C) Check which page has been around longer
D) Look for a way to verify which one is the real business

Answer below. Real answer dropping in the comments.

**Engagement strategy:**
- Pin a reply: "In Bantayan, the fake page had MORE likes (50,000) than the real one (12,000). More likes doesn't mean real. More professional photos doesn't mean real. Fast replies don't mean real. The only reliable check is verifying the business's identity through a source that scammers can't fake."
- Reply to each answer with the relevant data point
- Fear Activation phase: keep verification mention to the pinned comment, not the post body

**How this differs from C1 ("Spot the Fake"):** C1 uses a visual side-by-side comparison with actual page mockups. I3 uses a text-based scenario that drives opinion-based comments (people argue for their answer). Both drive engagement but through different mechanics — C1 is visual quiz, I3 is opinion debate.

# WHAT WAS CONSIDERED AND EXCLUDED

These formats were identified by the Facebook Sharing Playbook as high-performing but were excluded because they conflict with the marketing strategy's brand voice and positioning.

| Format | Why It Was Excluded |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Meme posts** | dotPH positions as an institutional authority ("the official .ph domain registry"). Meme tone works for lifestyle brands (Angkas, Netflix PH) but could undermine the trust signal that is dotPH's competitive advantage. The strategy's brand voice is "direct, empathetic, authoritative." |
| **Humor-forward content** | The strategy explicitly says "Never: sensationalize." While humor is the #1 Filipino sharing trigger, the campaign's subject matter (scams, financial loss, business impersonation) requires a protective and serious register. Self-deprecating brand humor doesn't fit a registry institution. |
| **Daily posting schedule** | The strategy specifies 3x/week (Mon/Wed/Fri), scaling to daily only in Weeks 6-8 with Stories. The playbook's recommended daily memes + 3-5x/week Reels is designed for community-building brands, not demand-creation campaigns. |
| **80/20 entertainment ratio** | The strategy's content pillars (40% Problem Education / 30% Solution / 30% Social Proof) are purpose-built for a 3-phase demand creation arc. Replacing 80% with entertainment content would undermine the campaign's conversion funnel. |
| **UGC-style Reels** | Requires real people on camera. Campaign uses AI-generated creatives. Phone-shot narration format not feasible. All Reels scripts use motion graphics and text animation instead. |
